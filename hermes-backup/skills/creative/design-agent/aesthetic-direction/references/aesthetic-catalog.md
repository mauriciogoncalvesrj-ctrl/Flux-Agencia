# Aesthetic Direction Catalog — 15 Curated Directions

Each aesthetic is a complete visual language. Use this as reference when presenting options to stakeholders.

---

## 1. Brutalist

**One-liner**: Raw, honest, unforgiving. No decoration. Pure form.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Stark contrast (pure black + white or minimal color)
- Thick, bold typography (often all-caps)
- Generous whitespace (emptiness is intentional)
- No gradients, shadows, or effects
- Geometric forms only
- Monochromatic or single-accent-color palettes
- Left-aligned, sometimes text-heavy

**Best for**: Design agencies, technical companies, B2B SaaS rebels, architectural firms, editorial publications, developers

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- brutalist.design — Meta. Is brutalist.
- abt-technology.com — Tech firm, stark positioning
- designbygrin.com — Design portfolio, demanding
- edenspiekermann.com — Design studio, no-frills

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Zodiak (geometric, heavy) or Recoleta (geometric, bold)
- Body: Inter (hyper-legible) or Erode (minimal)

**Color mood**: Monochromatic + one accent. Often navy/black + white + one primary color (teal, red, yellow). Zero saturation variation.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Grid-based, often single-column or two-column
- Lots of breathing room
- Typography is the primary visual element
- Heavy use of whitespace as design element
- Limited imagery
- Text-first approach

**Why choose Brutalism**:
- Differentiates in soft, colorful categories
- Signals "serious, demanding, no-nonsense"
- Works best with strong copywriting
- Maximum credibility in B2B
- Timeless (not trendy)

**Trade-offs**:
- Demands excellent content (weaknesses are exposed)
- Can feel cold or aggressive
- Requires confident brand voice
- Not suitable if you need warmth or playfulness
- Difficult to add personality later

## 2. Editorial / Magazine

**One-liner**: Authoritative, narrative-driven, image-forward. Like a high-end magazine.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Bold, high-contrast photography
- Generous margins and gutters
- Strong hierarchy through scale and weight
- Long-form narrative (articles, stories, captions)
- Grid-breaking image placement
- Rich typography with multiple weights
- Dark text on light backgrounds
- Serif or geometric headlines

**Best for**: Publishing, journalism, fashion, lifestyle brands, travel, luxury, agencies, nonprofits telling stories

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- theverge.com — Tech journalism, bold typography + photography
- dazeddigital.com — Fashion editorial, image-forward
- wallpapermag.com — Luxury publication, refined layout
- broadwayreview.com — Arts journalism, serif typography

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Clash Display (warm editorial) or Boska (luxury editorial)
- Body: General Sans (readable, editorial) or Erode (sophisticated)

**Color mood**: Cream/white backgrounds + one or two accent colors. Often uses rich blacks for hierarchy. Can incorporate color blocking.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Magazine-style grid (usually 2-3 column)
- Image placement drives layout (not rigid grid)
- Large whitespace around text blocks
- Sidebar or callout areas for secondary content
- Featured image area above fold
- Clear hierarchy of headlines, subheads, body, captions

**Why choose Editorial**:
- Signals authority and trust
- Excellent for storytelling
- Works with any industry
- Timeless (editorial design ages well)
- Naturally accommodates both text and imagery

**Trade-offs**:
- Requires strong photography
- Content-heavy (many words expected)
- Not suitable for minimal, image-only brands
- Can feel dated if colors are trendy
- Requires careful typography implementation

## 3. Luxury / Refined

**One-liner**: Elegant, precious, exclusive. Understated sophistication.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Minimal color (often monochromatic + gold/silver)
- Generous margins and spacing
- Serif typography for headlines
- Minimal sans-serif for body
- Fine, thin strokes
- Lots of negative space
- Premium photography or product photography
- Subtle textures (linen, paper, marble)
- Letter-spacing and kerning matter

**Best for**: High-end fashion, jewelry, luxury goods, premium real estate, fine dining, beauty, automotive

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- hermès.com — Luxury fashion, minimal + gold
- patek-philippe.com — Luxury watches, serif + serif
- chanel.com — Fashion, monochromatic + photography
- thestandard.com — Luxury hotel, refined & editorial hybrid

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Boska (serif, elegant) or Recoleta (serif, bold)
- Body: Erode (minimal, sophisticated) or Eudoxie Display (serif luxury)

**Color mood**: Monochromatic (black/navy/cream) + optional accent (gold, rose gold, green). Very low saturation.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Massive margins
- Single-column or two-column, never cramped
- Photography-first (images do the talking)
- Minimal text, every word counts
- Centered or right-aligned layout common
- White/cream background almost always

**Why choose Luxury**:
- Instantly communicates premium positioning
- Requires minimal words (good for limited copywriting)
- Timeless (luxury design ages best)
- Pairs perfectly with high-end photography
- Differentiates through absence, not presence

**Trade-offs**:
- Requires premium photography to support it
- Can feel empty if content is weak
- Difficult to add personality
- Works only in luxury categories (wrong for playful brands)
- Minimal color limits brand flexibility

## 4. Organic / Natural

**One-liner**: Flowing, botanical, hand-drawn. Nature-inspired, warm.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Curved, flowing forms (no right angles)
- Hand-drawn illustrations
- Botanical elements (leaves, branches, flowers)
- Warm color palette (earth tones, pastels)
- Organic typography (rounded, friendly)
- Asymmetrical layouts (intentional imbalance)
- Lots of greenery and nature imagery
- Soft shadows and blurred backgrounds

**Best for**: Wellness, healthcare, organic/natural brands, nonprofits, education, lifestyle, eco-friendly companies, agriculture

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- patagonia.com — Outdoor company, nature-integrated
- organicbasics.com — Sustainable fashion, warm & organic
- hemlibrary.com — Wellness, hand-drawn, curved
- forestry.io — Eco-tech, nature-forward

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Clash Display (rounded, warm) or Zodiak (bold organic)
- Body: General Sans (warm, readable) or Inter (clean, readable)

**Color mood**: Earth tones (sage, terracotta, ochre, cream, deep greens). Warm, muted saturation.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Curved section breaks and dividers
- Asymmetrical composition
- Flowing, non-grid layouts
- Illustrations break typography grid
- Whitespace follows organic shapes, not rectangles
- Photography of nature integrated throughout

**Why choose Organic**:
- Signals care, authenticity, natural/healthy
- Excellent for wellness and eco brands
- Warm and approachable
- Differentiates from corporate/sterile aesthetics
- Works well with hand-drawn content

**Trade-offs**:
- Can feel trendy or "wellness cliché"
- Difficult to feel serious or corporate
- Requires custom illustration
- Not suitable for tech/finance
- Colors can muddy if not carefully selected

## 5. Retro-Futuristic

**One-liner**: 80s nostalgia meets digital optimism. Neon + geometry.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Neon colors (hot pink, electric blue, lime green)
- Geometric patterns and grids
- 80s typography (sometimes pixel fonts)
- Layered, sometimes glitchy effects
- Gradient overlays
- Metallic or chrome effects
- High contrast, high saturation
- Retro photography or sci-fi imagery

**Best for**: Tech startups, gaming, music, entertainment, creative agencies, crypto, NFTs, metaverse projects

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- poolside.ai — AI company, neon retro aesthetic
- linear.app — Dev tool, dark + neon accents
- hyperdrive.digital — Digital agency, neon heavy
- vice.com — Media, neon accents with editorial

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Zodiak (geometric, heavy) or Satoshi (geometric, modern)
- Body: Inter (futuristic, clean) or Switzer (energetic)

**Color mood**: Neon primary colors (bright pink, bright cyan, bright lime). Often uses black or dark backgrounds. High saturation always.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Grid-based with intentional disruption
- Layered elements (text over images with effects)
- Gradient backgrounds or color blocks
- Geometric shapes as design elements
- High visual density
- Animated elements common

**Why choose Retro-Futuristic**:
- Instantly energetic and memorable
- Differentiates in serious categories
- Works perfectly for tech/creative brands
- Supports animated, interactive experiences
- Trendy (but in a intentional, self-aware way)

**Trade-offs**:
- Can feel gimmicky if not executed well
- May not age well (depends on execution)
- Wrong for serious/corporate brands
- Requires strong design execution
- Can be hard to read if colors clash

## 6. Maximalist Chaos

**One-liner**: Busy, textured, personality-heavy. Anti-minimal, unapologetically packed.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Multiple patterns and textures layered
- Busy color palette (5+ colors)
- Overlapping elements
- Lots of visual elements competing for attention
- Playful, eclectic typography mixes
- Collage-like layouts
- No negative space (intentional clutter)
- Illustrated or varied imagery

**Best for**: Creative agencies, design studios, artists, indie brands, entertainment, fashion-forward companies, rebellious D2C

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- awwwards.com — Design inspiration, maximalist gallery
- sosolimited.com — Creative studio, pattern-heavy
- yolélé.com — Food brand, vibrant + textured
- mailchimp.com/presents — Creative hub, eclectic + colorful

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Zodiak (bold, geometric) or any geometric font in large sizes
- Body: Switzer (energetic) or General Sans (neutral, readability anchor)

**Color mood**: 5-7 colors, high saturation, intentional color clashing. Often neons mixed with pastels.

**Layout philosophy**:
- No grid (or broken grid)
- Overlapping elements
- Varied image sizes and placements
- Multiple typeface families
- Shapes and patterns as background
- Animation and interaction reward exploration

**Why choose Maximalism**:
- Instantly memorable and unique
- Celebrates complexity and personality
- Perfect for creative/artistic brands
- Differentiates maximally from minimal competitors
- Supports storytelling through visual density

**Trade-offs**:
- Very difficult to execute well (usually looks messy)
- Readability can suffer
- Not suitable for serious, corporate, or trust-based brands
- Can overwhelm users if information hierarchy is poor
- Requires exceptional design skill

## 7. Playful / Toy-like

**One-liner**: Rounded, colorful, friendly. Like a children's picture book, but sophisticated.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Rounded corners on everything
- Bright, cheerful color palette
- Rounded or friendly typography
- Illustration-heavy
- Soft shadows
- Cute or whimsical elements
- Lowercase typography common
- Emoji-like illustrations

**Best for**: Kids products, creative tools, SaaS for creators, education apps, entertainment, D2C startups, design tools

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- figma.com — Design tool, rounded playful
- dropbox.com — Cloud storage, friendly playful
- intercom.com — Customer platform, rounded + playful
- kickpush.com — Skateboard brand, playful + bold

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Clash Display (rounded, warm) or Cabinet Grotesk (bold, geometric)
- Body: Switzer (friendly, energetic) or General Sans (warm)

**Color mood**: Bright, cheerful palette (coral, sky blue, sunny yellow, mint green). Medium saturation, warm tones.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Rounded corners on cards, buttons, images
- Generous spacing with soft, rounded visual breaks
- Asymmetrical composition
- Illustrations integrate into layout
- Whitespace feels playful, not minimal
- Micro-interactions and animation common

**Why choose Playful**:
- Instantly warm and approachable
- Works well for creative tools and education
- Good for D2C brands needing personality
- Illustration-friendly (can reduce reliance on photography)
- Supports interactive experiences

**Trade-offs**:
- Difficult to feel serious or corporate
- Can feel juvenile if not carefully executed
- Limited to specific industries
- Typography roundness can reduce readability at small sizes
- Custom illustration required for best results

## 8. Art Deco / Geometric

**One-liner**: Symmetrical, luxe, 1920s-inspired. Gold lines and structured geometry.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Perfect symmetry
- Gold, silver, or copper accents
- Strong geometric shapes (triangles, hexagons, circles)
- Serif or art-deco typography
- Dark backgrounds common (navy, black, jewel tones)
- Fine lines separating elements
- High contrast
- Pattern borders and ornamental elements

**Best for**: Luxury brands, jewelry, real estate, hospitality, finance, events, premium services

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- sapphirehotels.com — Luxury hotel, gold + geometry
- assouline.com — Luxury publishing, art deco aesthetic
- bulgari.com — Jewelry, symmetrical geometry
- morganstanley.com — Finance, refined geometry

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Recoleta (serif, bold, deco-friendly) or Boska (serif, elegant)
- Body: Erode (minimal, sophisticated) or Panchang (geometric, warm)

**Color mood**: Dark backgrounds (navy, black, deep jewel tones) + metallic accents (gold, rose gold, copper) + one bright accent.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Symmetrical composition
- Centered alignment
- Fine lines as design elements
- Geometric shapes integrated into layout
- Art deco patterns as borders or accents
- Ample spacing around geometric elements

**Why choose Art Deco**:
- Instantly luxurious and premium
- Timeless (1920s design remains elegant)
- Strong visual identity
- Works perfectly for luxury/premium brands
- Geometric approach aids responsive design

**Trade-offs**:
- Requires perfect execution (even small asymmetries break it)
- Limited to premium categories
- Geometric constraints limit flexibility
- Pattern-heavy can feel busy
- Serif typography requires careful implementation

## 9. Industrial / Utilitarian

**One-liner**: Technical, minimal, functional. Like an engineering blueprint.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Monochromatic or limited color (grays, steel, blacks)
- Geometric, technical typography
- Minimal ornamentation
- Functional layout (no decoration)
- Grid-based structure
- Technical illustrations or schematics
- Sometimes pixel-heavy or pixelated
- Hardware/mechanical imagery

**Best for**: Tools, technical products, industrial design, automotive, construction, engineering, IT

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- replicadesigns.com — Industrial design studio
- tesla.com — Automotive, technical minimal
- solidworks.com — CAD software, utilitarian
- carhartt.com — Workwear, industrial aesthetic

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Zodiak (geometric, heavy) or Inter (technical, clean)
- Body: Inter (hyper-legible) or General Sans (clean, neutral)

**Color mood**: Monochromatic (grays, blacks, whites) with optional one accent in primary color. Zero saturation, zero emotion.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Strict grid adherence
- Functional only (nothing decorative)
- Technical diagrams and schematics
- Minimal imagery or technical photography
- Whitespace for scanning, not beauty
- Accessibility paramount

**Why choose Industrial**:
- Communicates technical credibility
- Works perfectly for tools and technical products
- Functional approach aids usability
- Differentiates from design-heavy competitors
- Timeless (function doesn't go out of style)

**Trade-offs**:
- Can feel cold or uninviting
- Limited visual interest for brand differentiation
- Wrong for consumer/lifestyle brands
- Minimal color limits emotional connection
- Typography choices are limited

## 12. Swiss / International Grid

**One-liner**: Order, clarity, legibility. Swiss modernism maxed out.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Perfect grid adherence (8px, 12px, or 16px base)
- Minimal, geometric sans-serif
- Neutral color palette (grays, one primary)
- Perfect alignment
- Systematic spacing
- No decoration
- High information density
- Technical, precise layout

**Best for**: B2B SaaS, government, healthcare, finance, dashboards, technical documentation, corporate

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- ibm.com — Corporate, grid-perfect
- gov.uk — Government, Swiss grid system
- mailchimp.com — SaaS, grid-based
- twilio.com — API company, structured grid

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Inter (neutral grid-friendly) or Satoshi (geometric, minimal)
- Body: Inter (legible, neutral) or Erode (minimal, clean)

**Color mood**: Neutral palette (grays) with one primary color accent. Minimal saturation. High contrast for readability.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Strict 8px or 12px grid
- Perfect alignment to grid
- Generous margins and gutters
- Systematic whitespace
- Information hierarchy through weight and color
- No decorative elements

**Why choose Swiss Grid**:
- Maximum legibility and scannability
- Works perfectly for data and information
- Scales to any device seamlessly
- Timeless (Swiss design is 70+ years old)
- Supports complex information architecture

**Trade-offs**:
- Can feel rigid or cold
- Minimal visual personality
- Difficult to differentiate in category
- Requires perfect execution
- Not suitable for playful or emotional brands

## 13. Memphis / Postmodern

**One-liner**: Ironic, colorful, anti-serious. 90s nostalgia, intentionally weird.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Clashing, intentional color combinations
- Geometric shapes (circles, squiggles, impossible angles)
- Playful, sometimes jarring typography
- Mix of sans-serif and serif randomly
- Asymmetrical, rule-breaking layout
- Pattern-heavy
- Illustration that's intentionally naive or cartoonish
- Sometimes glitchy or pixelated

**Best for**: Creative agencies, startups, entertainment, design tools, indie brands, rebels in category

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- adidas.com — Adidas creative section, Memphis-inspired
- artshevik.com — Design studio, intentionally weird
- designreform.com — Design practice, playful rules-breaking
- postmorningstudio.com — Creative studio, chaotic playful

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Zodiak (bold, geometric) or Cabinet Grotesk (aggressive geometric)
- Body: Switzer (energetic, playful) or Panchang (warm, geometric)

**Color mood**: 4-6 clashing colors intentionally, high saturation, warm and cool mixed. Intentional discord.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Rules exist to be broken
- Asymmetrical, chaotic, intentional imbalance
- Shapes and patterns create structure not grid
- Typography size and weight vary wildly
- Animation adds playfulness

**Why choose Memphis**:
- Instantly memorable and unique
- Perfectly communicates "we don't take ourselves seriously"
- Differentiates maximally
- Works for rebellious brands
- Supports personality-forward positioning

**Trade-offs**:
- Very difficult to execute well (usually looks accidental)
- Information hierarchy can suffer
- Readability challenges
- Only works if brand personality matches
- Can feel dated quickly (depends on execution)

## 14. Japanese Minimal (Wabi-Sabi)

**One-liner**: Emptiness as design. Imperfection celebrated. Zen simplicity.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Maximum whitespace / emptiness
- Minimal color (often monochromatic)
- Handmade or slightly imperfect elements
- Asymmetrical balance (not centered)
- Fine, thin typography
- Negative space is key design element
- Often includes natural materials (wood, stone textures)
- Impermanence suggested (worn edges, aged appearance)

**Best for**: Luxury, fine art, philosophy, design studios, premium small goods, photography, peace/meditation apps

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- muji.com — Minimalist goods, wabi-sabi perfect
- nomadicmodern.com — Design studio, minimal
- isseymiyake.com — Fashion, refined minimal
- slowood.studio — Artisanal woodwork, imperfect elegant

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Recoleta (serif, minimal) or Boska (serif, elegant)
- Body: Erode (minimal, sophisticated) or General Sans (warm, readable)

**Color mood**: Monochromatic (black, white, grays) with optional one earth tone. Almost no color.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Extreme whitespace (emptiness is design)
- Asymmetrical balance
- One or two elements per section
- Imperfect or handmade quality
- Natural textures
- Negative space as prominent as positive space

**Why choose Wabi-Sabi**:
- Signals premium, artisanal positioning
- Instantly peaceful and meditative
- Rare in Western design (extreme differentiation)
- Supports slow, intentional brand positioning
- Works with minimal content (less is more)

**Trade-offs**:
- Extremely difficult to execute (requires deep understanding)
- Very limited audience (not mass market)
- Can feel empty or unfinished if not intentional
- Wrong for fast, energetic brands
- Requires exceptional photography/imagery

## 15. Neo-Brutalism (Rounded Brutalism)

**One-liner**: Bold and rounded. Honesty + friendliness. Brutalism with personality.

**Visual characteristics**:
- Rounded corners (not sharp)
- Bold, primary colors (not minimalist palette)
- Thick, bold typography
- Whitespace (though not as severe as Brutalism)
- Geometric, rounded shapes
- High contrast
- Honest, no decoration (but cheerful)
- Doodle-like illustrations sometimes

**Best for**: Startups, design tools, tech, creative companies, apps, D2C brands, modern SaaS

**Example websites** (see craftwork.design):
- linear.app — Project management, rounded bold
- superwall.me — Startup, rounded geometric
- brevo.com — SaaS, rounded primary colors
- coolify.io — Dev tool, rounded bold blocks

**Recommended Fontshare fonts**:
- Display: Cabinet Grotesk (bold, geometric) or Zodiak (geometric, heavy)
- Body: Inter (modern, legible) or Switzer (friendly, energetic)

**Color mood**: Bold primary colors (teal, coral, purple) with dark backgrounds and whitespace. High contrast, high saturation.

**Layout philosophy**:
- Rounded corners on all shapes
- Bold color blocks as background
- Generous whitespace mixed with bold elements
- Geometric forms (rounded squares, circles)
- Typography-focused but with personality
- Balance between bold and empty

**Why choose Neo-Brutalism**:
- Combines honesty (Brutalism) with personality (Playful)
- Differentiates from soft pastels
- Modern and startup-forward
- Works well for tech and creative
- Bold colors create memorability

**Trade-offs**:
- Trendy right now (may feel dated in 2-3 years)
- Requires confident color selection
- Limited to tech/startup categories
- Difficult to feel premium or established
- Execution must be clean or it looks messy

## Implementation Workflows

Once you've chosen your aesthetic, use these related skills in order:

1. **`aesthetic-direction`** (you are here) — Pick visual direction
2. **`brand-research`** — Analyze competitors' aesthetics, identify gaps
3. **`typography-pairing`** — Select fonts matching your aesthetic
4. **`color-system`** — Generate full palette from your aesthetic's color mood
5. **`design-system-generator`** — Build complete design system (tokens, components, spacing)
6. **`ad-creative-design`** — Apply aesthetic to paid advertising
7. **`component-patterns`** — Build UI components aligned with aesthetic
